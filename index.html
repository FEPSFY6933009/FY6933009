Abstract

The rise in awareness of sustainability (the avoidance of the depletion of natural resources to maintain ecological balance) is becoming increasingly prevalent as the climate continues to worsen, and extreme weather events are also becoming higher in intensity and frequency. Extreme weather events are rare occasions of weather patterns which deviate from usual weather. For example, tsunamis and wildfires. Despite being the most widely used building material, concrete is an unnoticeable contributor to climate change. The carbon emissions from concrete help to raise the global temperature as well as ruin animal ecosystems through the extraction of sand from rivers and coasts. However, there's a chance that the future may lead to a rise in sustainable alternatives, moving society one step closer to net-zero. This is where the amount of harmful gases emitted into the atmosphere is equal to the amount removed from it.

Analysis and discussion

Concrete's carbon contributions

Concrete had its first major 'boom' in usage during post-World War Two development. To this day, major cities, scuh as Birmingham are defined by large concrete structures. Concrete is the source of about 8% of the world's carbon dioxide emissions (Lehne, J. and Preston, F. (2018)) The reason why carbon dioxide emission is bad is because when it's released into the atmosphere, it gets trapped inside of the ozone layer, acting as a blanket which doesn't let heat escape, causing the raise in global temperature (also known as global warming). Within the last 70+ years, rural-to-urban migration has significantly increased in major cities such as London. Rural-to-urban migration is defined as the movement of people from countryside areas, to cities. This is evident as from 1991 to 2022, the population increased from 6.8 million to 8.9 million and is projected to reach 9.3 million in 2025 (Trust For London(2024)) This increase has led to higher demand for housing, which has been met as housing programmes have resulted in 38,308 new houses between 1 April 2024 and 31 March 2025, representing an increase by 5% compared to the same period the previous year (England, H. (2025)) Therefore, the higher demand for housing to accomodate the higher population has resulted in the need for more building materials, causing concrete use to surge further.

Consequences on resources and ecosystems

Concrete has a life span of around 50-100 years (FMP Construction Marketing(2025)) This means that it must be replaced often, leading to further carbon emissions through repeating the process of making the concrete and extracting more sand. One reason for the short life span is weathering processes. Weathering is when a material is worn down due to exposure to the atmosphere. Chemical weathering wears down concrete as acid rain breaks it down and reduces its life span. Furthermore, freeze-thaw weathering can also reduce the life span. This is because water seeps into any cracks in the concrete, then the water freezes, causing it to expand, along with the crack. Overtime the size of the crack increases and damages the structure, meaning it must be repaired. (Mizer, J.K. (2025) As well as this, the extraction of sand destroys animal ecosystems

Brighter perspective

There are an increasing number of greener alternatives. For example, using green sand. Jason, Lee, B.D. and Dungan, R.S. (2008). Green sands are commonly known as clay-bonded sand and are an alternative to using natural sand. This is because it uses industrial waste rather than river sand, therefore waste becomes a resource rather than another addition to landfill. Furthermore, as less sand is extracted animal habitats are preserved which benefits many ecosystems. As well as this, when comparing concrete to other materials like steel, positives include the fact that concrete is a less energy-intensive resource. This means that less energy is used in the process of making concrete, rather than making reinforcing steel. This reduces impact on the climate as energy is typically harnessed through burning gas and coal, which releases carbon into the atmosphere. In addition, innovative ways have been discovered in order to increase the lifespan of concrete, including making it hydrophobic Sindhu,J., & Kumar, P.(2023) so that weathering processes, such as freeze-thaw weathering, can't wear it away as quickly.

Conclusion

Can concrete emissions decrease? Concrete is still an essential part in the world of construction but the environment costs are dire. As of now, it's unsustainable due to its negatives effects on ecosystem, current high carbon emissions, and its strain on resources, including natural sand. However, the alternatives options may develop in the near future, increasing the durability and longevity of concrete. Nevertheless, this will require strict regulations, as well as global cooperation and responsibility.
